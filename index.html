What makes a YouTube Video Trend?

By Justin Chen and Evan McIntire

Youtube is one of the most visited websites in the world, with over 30 million visiters per day, and 5 billion videos watched a day. Youtube showcases videos that are currently popular on their Trending videos page. From the perspective of a content creator, having a trending video is considered an accomplishment, and can bring a lot of revenue and attention. Advertisers also like trending videos and will often pay more to have their ads shown in hopes of reaching a wider audience.

There are a lot of other, independent forces and trends that affect how popular Youtube videos will become. However, due to given such as tags, categories, and collected data such as views and likes, we can attempt to quantify what constitutes a trending video from a data science perspective. This type of data is not only interesting to examine and interpret, but can be very valuable to advertisers.

Using a dataset that contains the trending videos from November 2017 to June 2018, as well as category metadata, can we discren any factors that these videos have in common, and then use that to try to predict how succesful other videos will be, and what level of engagement they will get?

Next, we're going to tidy up the US Trending Youtube Video Dataset.

For the purposes of our analysis, we don't really care about the video_id,publish_time, thumbnail_link, comments_disabled, ratings_disabled, video_error_or_removed, or description columns of our dataset, so we'll drop them.

We also want to convert the publish_time and trending_date of each row into datetime objects so they're easier to work with later.

In additon, we want tags as an array rather than a big string, so we can explore and see if tags have any relationship with trending videos.

In [5]:
for index, row in yt_df.iterrows():
    # Convert trending date and publish time into python datetimes
    yt_df.at[index, 'trending_date'] = datetime.strptime(row['trending_date'], '%y.%d.%m')

    yt_df.at[index, 'tags'] = row['tags'].replace('"','').split("|")

# Number of days trending
days_trending = yt_df.groupby('video_id').size()

# Max views during those days
max_views = yt_df.groupby('video_id')['views'].max()

yt_df = yt_df.drop_duplicates('video_id')
for index, row in yt_df.iterrows():
    yt_df.at[index, 'views'] = max_views[row['video_id']]
    yt_df.at[index, 'days_trending'] = days_trending[row['video_id']]

User Engagement¶

As mentioned before, advertisers are a large stakeholder in Youtube, especially when it comes to trending videos.

Content creators encourge users to "like, comment, and subscribe" all the time, because it drives user engagement - things like views, comments, and likes. Advertisers value engagement over raw views, as they tend to lead to higher clickthrough and reach of the ads.

We're going to do analysis to define a reasonable user engagement score, and then use regression to test the following null hypothesis: There is no relationship between category and engagement.

Defining user engagement¶

Any interaction with the video is considered engagement, but some forms are more rare, and more valuable. Views are the most common, followed by ratings, with comments coming in last place.

As a naive measurement of engagement, we're first going to normalize these metrics against each other, and then score each video as a sum of the normalized metrics.

So, we can conclude that there is a correlation between category and user engagement.

Conclusion¶

First, we looked at how people react to trending videos. In general, they get a lot of views, with some being breakaway hits. Looking at likes and dislikes, most trending videos have a high like-to-dislike ratio, but some actually have more dislikes than likes.

Then, we looked at data about the videos themselves - what are the categories? The tags? Entertainment and music both tended to be very represented in trending, while for tags humor was the most frequent.

Then, we defined a metric of user engagement and asked if there was a correlation between engagement and category. After doing a linear regression, we found a very significant relationship.

For content creators and advertisers, this is huge. Both parties benefit from involving themselves in these high-engagement categories, and likely do data science similar to this.

Additional Resources¶

There's so much more than can be done in this space, as long as the data is out there. This analysis focused on metadata, rather than on the actual content of the videos themselves. While we don't have full data on that, official datasets like youtube8m are a fantastic start to bring the actual content into the fold in an automated way.

There's also a lot of other analysis that could be done, given the data we have. Do some channels trend more than others? Can we use the titles and descriptions in a meaningful way, perhaps with NLP? If we weight other statistics using views, does our analysis change?
